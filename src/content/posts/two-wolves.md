### Opening Pandora's Box

When I was twelve, I discovered how to jailbreak[^jailbreak] my iPhone 4. That very night, instead of sleeping, I spent hours under my blanket customizing the user interface. My child self was captivated by the seemingly endless possibilities. Needless to say, this experience decided my obsession for the next few months. Whether it be on the car ride home, at church, or wherever I found myself to be, I would spend my time browsing Cydia[^cydia], looking for cool packages to try. Unfortunately, the honeymoon period was soon to end. One day, I thought that the "Siri" assistant that came with the newer iPhone models was reaaaaalllllly cool, so I tried downloading a package that claimed to add it.

**It bricked my phone.**

I felt slighted. Whoever published that package probably had a good chuckle at ruining the day of a child who had barely seen his thirteenth summer. After reconciling with the fact that there was nothing to be done, I factory reset the phone and went right back to tinkering. Albeit discouraging, I had one of my first true tastes of technological curiosity.

---

Jailbreaking my iPhone, downloading Pokemon on my calculator, building my own computer.

Growing up, I was one of those kids who was fascinated with every piece technology. Learning the ins and outs of a device provided me a level of satisfaction that was seldom found elsewhere. I suspect for little me, it offered a space where I felt like I was in total control, a place just for me; somewhere I could go back to when recouping from the outside world, a place where so many things were outside my control. It was a place I felt like I could understand[^author-note-1].

I think those sparks of curiosity were the ember that blazed into my career in the tech field.
